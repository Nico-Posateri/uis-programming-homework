Van Jacobson and TCP/IP

Early Life

Save for the fact that Van Jacobson was born circa 1950 and his interest in high school physics, very little has been made public about his life pre-university.

Education

Jacobson engaged in graduate work at the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory as he finished his computer science doctorate.
He credits his unique deductive reasoning to his time spent working on control software for linear accelerators. Through this, he claims
to have learned how to ask the right questions.

Career Highlights

By October 1986, after an extensive break from computer science and physics, Jacobson decided to get back to work.
Working closely with Mike Karels to develop Slow-Start, the Berkeley Unix developers attempted to solve the Internet's congestion gridlock, which
had reduced traffic speeds from ~56K bit/sec to ~320 bit/sec. He spent nearly six months trying to figure out how to reduce congestion before
falling back on his physicist methodology: asking the right question would reveal the solution.

Rather than asking himself how he could make this work, he thought, "How was the internet ever able to work in the first place?" A solution quickly fell into place as he asserted that the Internet's standard technique of retransmitting information until it finally went through
was to blame for the severe congestion. Overcoming this obstacle was simply a matter of engineering a little "politeness" into the system.

The Van Jacobson Technique (Slow-Start) delayed the sending of packets from one device by a few milliseconds until the network was clear of packets
which were already in transit. This technique was first built into Berkeley Unix servers in 1987, but it is also largely to thank for the modern internet's
congestion-free state. Interestingly, the number of users was ~10,000 at that time. As of 2024, there are ~5.44 billion internet users worldwide.
